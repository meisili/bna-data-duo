The Chemistry of Beauty

An investigation into the hidden chemical components of your daily cosmetic routine.

The Cost of Your Routine

Many people use beauty products as part of their daily routines. American adults use about 12 personal care products a day, according to Consumer Report.

Whether it’s a lipstick, perfume, or even moisturizer – many of these products are made with harmful chemicals. These chemicals can cause serious health issues. Many are carcinogenic and can cause different kinds of cancer. Others are toxic to the reproductive system and the immune system.

Some ingredients, like formaldehyde have been in the news for the past several years, and consumers have started to recognize and avoid the toxic chemical.

  • Beauty products marketed to Black women contain toxic formaldehyde
  • Cancer-causing chemicals are in many beauty products women use, a study finds

Yet many other harmful ingredients in cosmetic products aren’t as recognizable as Formaldehyde.

Despite the known linkages of these chemicals to bodily harm, the federal government has allowed them to continue to be used in the market and hasn’t passed any legislation to require transparent labeling on products.

California has banned the most chemicals of any state – 25 – and is the only state to require cosmetic companies to report harmful ingredients in their products.

Deep Dive: What's Under the Surface?

In 2005, the California state government passed the California Safe Cosmetics Act. The bill went into effect in 2007, and since then, over 165,000 products have been submitted to the state government. 14% of these products reported have since been discontinued.

Cosmetic Reporting Timeline

Since 2009, California has required cosmetic companies to report harmful ingredients in their products.

The dips in the following years aren't because the new legislation led to fewer harmful ingredients. Companies rushed to comply with the new legislation when it first passed. They only need to report one time, so there were fewer submissions in the years that followed.

In 2022, California's Fragrance Ingredient Right to Know Act required companies to disclose fragrance ingredients for the first time — causing a dramatic surge in product submissions.
